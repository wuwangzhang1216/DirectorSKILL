# Cinematic Language Reference

Use this file when the task requires shot design, camera language, or director-level visual reasoning.

## Shot functions

| Function | Typical use | Good for | Avoid when |
|---|---|---|---|
| Establishing shot | Define location, scale, atmosphere | Opening, transition, isolation | The scene already has clear geography |
| Relation shot | Show power/relationship between characters | Dialogue, conflict, social pressure | Need internal psychology instead |
| Close-up | Show decision, fear, realization | Emotional turns | Overusing every line |
| Insert/detail | Make object/story clue meaningful | Suspense, memory, irony | Object is not story-relevant |
| Reaction shot | Show consequence of action/line | Comedy, horror, social tension | Reaction repeats previous emotion |
| Transition shot | Move time/place/emotion | Dream logic, memory, montage | It merely looks pretty |
| Aftermath shot | Let audience process | Horror, tragedy, satire | Need pace/urgency |

## Shot sizes

- Extreme wide shot: geography, loneliness, human smallness.
- Wide shot: relationship between body and environment.
- Medium shot: readable action and dialogue.
- Medium close-up: performance with context.
- Close-up: thought, judgment, fear, decision.
- Extreme close-up: obsession, clue, bodily detail, psychological pressure.

## Camera angles

- Eye level: realism, neutrality, observational tone.
- Low angle: threat, authority, myth, intimidation.
- High angle: weakness, vulnerability, judgment.
- Overhead: fate, system, layout, ritual, surveillance.
- Dutch angle: instability, dream, moral distortion. Use sparingly.
- Over-the-shoulder: confrontation, subjectivity, social pressure.

## Camera movement

- Locked/static: control, dread, ritual, awkwardness, satire.
- Slow push-in/dolly-in: pressure, realization, hidden truth emerging.
- Slow pull-back/dolly-out: abandonment, isolation, aftermath.
- Pan: reveal, scan, connect two visual ideas.
- Tilt: reveal scale, authority, vertical power.
- Tracking/follow: journey, pursuit, embodied movement.
- Handheld: instability, panic, documentary immediacy.
- Orbit/arc: transformation, seduction, disorientation, power shift.
- Crane/boom: scale, release, fate, transcendence.

AI video rule: one dominant camera move per generated clip unless the model has explicit camera-control UI for combined moves.

## Lighting logic

- Low-key lighting: threat, secrecy, moral ambiguity, horror.
- Hard sidelight: judgment, harsh social order, noir, authority.
- Backlight/silhouette: myth, unknowability, hidden identity.
- Practical light: realism and motivated old-world atmosphere, e.g. oil lamp, candle, neon, streetlight.
- Soft overcast: melancholy, realism, emotional openness.
- Flicker: instability, fear, unreliable perception.

## Composition patterns

- Negative space: absence, threat, loneliness.
- Frame within frame: confinement, social pressure, surveillance.
- Symmetry: ritual, authoritarian order, absurdity, emotional deadness.
- Off-center subject: unease, uncertainty, imbalance.
- Foreground obstruction: secrecy, voyeurism, partial knowledge.
- Deep staging: power layers, social hierarchy, memory spaces.

## Editing rhythm

- Long static hold: dread, discomfort, satire.
- Hard cut: shock, irony, decision, rupture.
- Match cut: thematic connection across time/space.
- Slow dissolve: memory, dream, decay, transformation.
- Jump cut: mental discontinuity, compression, absurdity.

## Director's rule

Every camera decision must answer: “What should the audience feel or understand now that they could not feel or understand before?”
